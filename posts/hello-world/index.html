Hello, World?

Not an intriguing topic to write as my first story. Please bear with me as I just like to start murmuring something that emerges in my mind, for no particular reason. Is it the reason many, if not most, bloggers do? I’ll have to image so. So don’t tell me your answer.

Well, I don’t remember in what language I write my first “hello world” program. My best guess would be Pascal or C, as I don’t recall doing so using Basic on my first Apple II computer.

These days, however, I write hello-world programs regularly, in an embarrassingly high frequency. I do know the main reason: “Why the hack there are so many new tech-kids on the block?” I’m sure it is the main reason for most people (modulo those guys writing get-started guides). I’ll have to image so. So don’t tell me your answer either.

As a non-native English speaking person, what I really want to murmur today is this minor inquiry: “What is the actual text you print in your hello world program(s)?” Well, my absent-minded usual choices are:

  • hello world
  • hello, world
  • Hello, World
  • Hello World
  • Hello, world.

Ignoring the grammatical hiccups, only recently had I become aware of the “more authenticated” versions:

  • hello, world!
  • Hello, world!

Well, I know it is not a big deal (or is it?). If I had a smarter spelling checker I would have been annoyed with some red wavy underline earlier. But my inner voice (hence the murmuring) emerges anyway: “Why the exclamation!”

  • Does it want to come out and greet the world so badly?
  • Does it think “what takes you so long” and just want to tease me?
  • Does it sense my loneliness and want to lighten the atmosphere?

If so, maybe I should change the text to “Hello, ${USER}<3”, or something more aggressive? But I guess it’s unwise to over do it in such an open-source world (even with commitment to a private repo?)

What was in the “C Programming Language” author’s mind? Well, we all can google it and get an approximation. I do enjoy the yearn for creating something lively and friendly, doing no harm.
